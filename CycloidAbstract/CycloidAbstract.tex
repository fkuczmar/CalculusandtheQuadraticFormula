\begin{exploration} \label{SlidingLadders}
There is exactly one point $P$ on the quarter-ellipse
\[
 \frac{x^2}{u^2} + \frac{y^2}{v^2} = 1 \, , \, x,y\geq 0,
\]
where the tangent segments $\overline{PA}$ and $\overline{PB}$ from $P$ to the coordinate axes have lengths $PA=v$ and $PB=u$ equal to the semi-axes. As illustrated in the figure below, this \emph{transition point} divides the quarter-ellipse into arc lengths $PA=s_x$ and $PB=s_y$ whose difference $s_y - s_x$ is equal to the difference  $u-v$ in the semi-axes.

%At this point the difference $s_y - s_x$ in the arc lengths $PY$ and $PX$ of the ellipse from $P$ to the coordinate axes equals the difference $u-v$ in the lengths of the semi-axes. This paper is partly about proving this theorem. But it is mostly about taking a local approach to this and other calculus problems.

The first step in understanding why is to animate the figure, either by dragging the slider $\phi$ in Line 2 or pressing the play button. Then you'll see the endpoints of segment $\overline{AB}$ and another segment $\overline{CD}$ slide along the coordinate axes as the point $P$, attached to the longer segment $\overline{AB}$ (length $u+v$) and to an extension of the shorter $\overline{CD}$ (length $u-v$), sweeps out the quarter-ellipse. 

You can drag the sliders $a=(u-v)/2$ (Line 7) and $b=(u+v)/2$ (Line 8) to change the shape and size of the ellipse.

The circle on diameter $CD=2a = u-v$ helps establish the arc length equality in Theorem 1. The circle on diameter $AB = 2b = u+v$ helps identify the transition point separating the negative and positive parts of the arc length.

Play the slider $\phi_2$ in Line 2 to complete the paths as the circle on diameter $\overline{CD}$ rolls in the reference frame of the fixed circle (top) and in the reference frame of the rolling tangent line (bottom).

%Because the rolling circle has half the radius of the fixed, it rotates half as fast in the top frame as it does in the bottom. So any point at rest in the reference frame of the rolling circle moves half as fast in the top frame as it does in the bottom. Thus,

Because the circle rotates twice as fast in the second frame as it does in the first, any point at rest in the reference frame of the rolling circle moves twice as fast in the second frame as it does in the first. So at any stage of the motion, the length $A_2P_2$
of the prolate cycloid is twice the corresponding length $AP$ of the ellipse and the length $Z_2C_2$ of the ordinary cycloid is twice the length of segment $\overline{ZC}$.

Proving Theorem 1 then essentially amounts to showing the difference in the lengths of the prolate cycloid above and below ${\cal L}_2$ equals the length of the ordinary cycloid.

\begin{exploration} \label{CurtateProlateCyloidsRolling}
The figure below shows the paths traced by the point $P$ in the reference frames of the rolling tangent lines of Exploration 2. The first path is one arch of a curtate cycloid, where $P$ is inside the rolling circle (radius $b = (u+v)/2$), at a distance $a=(u-v)/2$ units from its center. The second, dual to the first, is one arch of a  prolate cycloid, where $P$ is outside the rolling circle (radius $a$), $b$ units from its center. The third is an ordinary $a$-cycloid, traced by a point on a rolling circle with radius $a$. 

Proving Theorem 1 amounts to showing the arc lengths $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$ of the prolate cycloid above and below ${\cal L}_2$ respectively equal the arc lengths $s_1$, $s_2$ of the concave-up and concave-down arcs of the curtate cycloid, and that the difference $\sigma_1 - \sigma_2 = s_1-s_2$ equals the arc length $s_3$ of the $a$-cycloid.

Play the slider $u$ in Line 1 to see the circles roll in the reference frames of the rolling tangent lines ${\cal L}_1$ and ${\cal L}_2$. The first two circles rotate at the same rate and because the arc lengths $A_1P_1$ and $A_2P_2$ are each twice the arc length $XP$ of the semi-ellipse in Exploration 2,  $A_1P_1=A_2P_2$.
