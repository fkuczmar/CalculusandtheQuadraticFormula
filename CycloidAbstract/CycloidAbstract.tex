\begin{exploration} \label{SlidingLadders}
There is exactly one point $P$ (what we call the \emph{transition point}) on the quarter-ellipse
\[
 \frac{x^2}{u^2} + \frac{y^2}{v^2} = 1 \, , \, x,y\geq 0,
\]
where the tangent segments $\overline{PA}$ and $\overline{PB}$ from $P$ to the coordinate axes have lengths $PA=v$ and $PB=u$ equal to the semi-axes. At this point the difference $s_y - s_x$ in the arclengths $PY$ and $PX$ of the ellipse from $P$ to the coordinate axes equals the difference $u-v$ in the lengths of the semi-axes. This paper is partly about proving this theorem. But it is mostly about thinking differentially and taking a local approach to this and other calculus problems.

Animate the figure below, either by dragging the slider $\phi$ in Line 2 or pressing the play button in that line and you'll see the endpoints of segments $\overline{AB}$ and $\overline{CD}$ slide along the coordinate axes as the point $P$, attached to the longer segment $\overline{AB}$ and to an extension of the shorter $\overline{CD}$, sweeps out the quarter-ellipse. 

Drag the sliders $a=(u-v)/2$ and $b=(u+v)/2$ to change the shape and size of the ellipse.

\begin{exploration} \label{CurtateProlateCyloidsStatic}
The third animation shows the motions of the rolling circles and the tracing point $P$ in the reference frames of their rolling tangent lines. The first path is one arch of a curtate cycloid, traced by a point $a<b$ units from the center of a rolling circle with radius $b$. The second is the dual of the first, one arch of a  prolate cycloid, traced by a point $b>a$ units from the center of a rolling circle with radius $a$. The third is an ordinary $a$-cycloid, traced by point on a rolling circle with radius $a$. 

Proving Theorem 1 amouts to showing the difference $s_1-s_2$ in the arclengths of the prolate cycloid on the same and opposite sides of the fixed line equals the arclength $s_3$ of the $a$-cycloid. The difference in the concave up and concave down arclengths of the curate cycloid is the same. In fact, these arclengths are respectively equal to the same and opposite side arclengths of its dual prolate cycloid.

Drag sliders $a$ and $b$ in LInes 2 and 3 to change the shapes and sizes of the cycloids.

%Figure 1 shows a \emph{prolate cycloid} (from the Latin \emph{poferre}, to extend and the Greek \emph{kyklos}, circle). It is the curve traced by a point $P_1$ outside a circle rolling on a line

Roll a circle ${\cal C}_1$ of radius $a$ once through its circumference on a line ${\cal L}$ and a point $P_1$ fixed in the reference frame of the circle sweeps out one arch of a \emph{general cylcoid}. When $P_1$ is outside the circle, the \emph{prolate cylcoid} (from the Latin \emph{poferre}, to extend and the Greek \emph{kyklos}, circle) crosses ${\cal L}$. In a recent article,  D. Robinson showed the one-arch length of the prolate cycloid on the same side of ${\cal L}$ as ${\cal C}_1$ exceeds the opposite-side length by $8a$. The excess surprisingly does not depend on the distance $b>a$ of the tracing point from the center of ${\cal C}_1$.  Choosing $b=a$ shows the excess equals the one-arch length of the \emph{ordinary} $a$-cycloid traced by a point on the circumference of the same rolling circle (see Figure 1, where ${\cal L}$ is the $x$-axis, $a=2$, and $b=3.6$).
