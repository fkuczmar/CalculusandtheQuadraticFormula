\begin{exploration} \label{SlidingLadders}
There is exactly one point $P$ on the quarter-ellipse
\[
 \frac{x^2}{u^2} + \frac{y^2}{v^2} = 1 \, , \, x,y\geq 0,
\]
where the tangent segments $\overline{PA}$ and $\overline{PB}$ from $P$ to the coordinate axes have lengths $PA=v$ and $PB=u$ equal to the semi-axes. As illustrated in the figure below, this \emph{transition point} divides the quarter-ellipse into arclengths $PA=s_x$ and $PB=s_y$ whose difference $s_y - s_x$ is equal to the difference  $u-v$ in the semi-axes.

%At this point the difference $s_y - s_x$ in the arclengths $PY$ and $PX$ of the ellipse from $P$ to the coordinate axes equals the difference $u-v$ in the lengths of the semi-axes. This paper is partly about proving this theorem. But it is mostly about taking a local approach to this and other calculus problems.

Animate the figure, either by dragging the slider $\phi$ in Line 2 or pressing the play button, and you'll see the endpoints of segment $\overline{AB}$ and another $\overline{CD}$ slide along the coordinate axes as the point $P$, attached to the longer segment $\overline{AB}$ (length $u+v$) and to an extension of the shorter $\overline{CD}$ (length $u-v$), sweeps out the quarter-ellipse. 

You can drag the sliders $a=(u-v)/2$ (Line 7) and $b=(u+v)/2$ (Line 8) to change the shape and size of the ellipse.

\begin{exploration}  \label{EllipseProlateCycloid}
Play the slider $\phi_2$ in Line 2 to see the circle on diameter $\overline{CD}$ rolling in the reference frames of the fixed circle (radius $2a$) and the rolling tangent line.

Because the rolling circle has half the radius of the fixed circle, it rotates half as fast in the first frame as it does in the second. So any point at rest in the reference frame of the rolling circle moves half as fast in the first frame as it does in the second and
\[
  \text{arc}(XP) =0.5 \text{arc}(X_2P_2)  \hskip 0.3 in \text{and} \hskip 0.3 in \text{arc}(ZC) = \frac{1}{2}\text{arc}(Z_2C_2).
\]

%Figure 1 shows a \emph{prolate cycloid} (from the Latin \emph{poferre}, to extend and the Greek \emph{kyklos}, circle). It is the curve traced by a point $P_1$ outside a circle rolling on a line

Roll a circle ${\cal C}_1$ of radius $a$ once through its circumference on a line ${\cal L}$ and a point $P_1$ fixed in the reference frame of the circle sweeps out one arch of a \emph{general cylcoid}. When $P_1$ is outside the circle, the \emph{prolate cylcoid} (from the Latin \emph{poferre}, to extend and the Greek \emph{kyklos}, circle) crosses ${\cal L}$. In a recent article,  D. Robinson showed the one-arch length of the prolate cycloid on the same side of ${\cal L}$ as ${\cal C}_1$ exceeds the opposite-side length by $8a$. The excess surprisingly does not depend on the distance $b>a$ of the tracing point from the center of ${\cal C}_1$.  Choosing $b=a$ shows the excess equals the one-arch length of the \emph{ordinary} $a$-cycloid traced by a point on the circumference of the same rolling circle (see Figure 1, where ${\cal L}$ is the $x$-axis, $a=2$, and $b=3.6$).
